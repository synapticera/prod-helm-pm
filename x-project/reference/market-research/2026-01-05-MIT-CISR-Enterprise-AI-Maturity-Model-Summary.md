# MIT CISR Enterprise AI Maturity Model 2024-2025

## Summary & Key Insights for Helm

**Research Conducted**: MIT CISR (Center for Information Systems Research)
**Date of Analysis**: 2024-2025
**Sample Size**: 721 companies + 9 in-depth case studies
**Key Focus**: Enterprise AI maturity progression, financial impact, and agentic AI integration

---

## Executive Summary

MIT CISR's enterprise AI maturity research reveals a **critical profitability inflection** at Stage 3, where companies transition from pilot-focused experimentation to scaled AI ways of working. This directly validates Helm's positioning: the executive function layer is essential for moving enterprises from failed pilots to successful, profitable AI deployment.

**Key Statistic**: Enterprises in Stage 3-4 achieve **+11-17pp growth advantage** and **+8-10pp profit advantage** over industry average, while Stage 1-2 enterprises underperform by 2-12pp.

---

## The Four-Stage Enterprise AI Maturity Model

### Stage 1: Experiment & Prepare (28% of enterprises)

**Financial Performance**: -12.6pp growth, -9.6pp profit vs. industry average
**Characteristics**:

- Educational focus on AI literacy
- Early governance framework development
- Data accessibility initiatives
- Workforce training programs
- **Status**: Below-industry financial performance

**Why Enterprises Get Stuck Here**:

- Lack of clear success metrics
- Limited organizational alignment
- Uncertainty about AI application priority
- Governance frameworks incomplete

### Stage 2: Build Pilots & Capabilities (34% of enterprises)

**Financial Performance**: -3.5pp growth, -2.2pp profit vs. industry average
**Characteristics**:

- Demonstrating AI value through pilots
- Use case development and prioritization
- API integration and data consolidation
- Testing specific AI applications
- **Status**: Still underperforming, but closer to breakeven

**The Stage 2 Problem** (This is where Helm enters):

- Pilots work in isolation (not connected to broader strategy)
- Success metrics aren't tied to business outcomes
- Organization doesn't understand decision context
- Lack of transparency prevents stakeholder buy-in
- No governance layer to manage multiple agents
- **Result**: 33% of pilots fail; organizations can't explain why

### Stage 3: Develop AI Ways of Working (31% of enterprises in 2022 → 46% in 2025)

**Financial Performance**: +11.3pp growth, +8.7pp profit vs. industry average
**Characteristics**:

- Scaling AI infrastructure (moving beyond pilots)
- Industrializing deployments (repeatable processes)
- Platform-based approach (not one-off projects)
- Test-and-learn culture embedded
- Business intelligence dashboards connected to AI outcomes
- **Status**: Significantly outperforming industry average

**The Inflection Point**:

- This is where enterprises shift from "can we deploy AI?" to "how do we govern and optimize AI?"
- MIT research shows this is THE critical transition where financial returns materialize
- Organizations have moved from experimentation to operational execution

**What Makes Stage 3 Work**:

1. **Unified C-Suite Leadership**: CEO, CIO, Chief Strategy Officer, HR aligned on AI strategy
2. **Governance Structures**: Decision frameworks, oversight mechanisms, compliance guardrails
3. **Organizational Design**: Reskilled workforce, new roles (AI managers, decision architects)
4. **Platform Mentality**: AI infrastructure as shared, reusable foundation (not point solutions)
5. **Outcome Measurement**: Clear KPIs connected to business value

### Stage 4: Become AI Future Ready (7% of enterprises in 2022 → 18% in 2025)

**Financial Performance**: +17.1pp growth, +10.4pp profit vs. industry average
**Characteristics**:

- AI embedded in core decision-making
- New AI-based revenue streams
- Autonomous agents operating with minimal human oversight
- Proprietary AI development capabilities
- **Status**: Highest-performing segment, pulling away from competition

### 3. Financial Impact Timing

The MIT research shows a **major financial inflection between Stage 2 and Stage 3**:

```
Stage 1: -12.6pp growth | -9.6pp profit
Stage 2: -3.5pp growth | -2.2pp profit
              ↓ (Helm helps here)
Stage 3: +11.3pp growth | +8.7pp profit  ← 24pp swing in profitability
Stage 4: +17.1pp growth | +10.4pp profit
```

**For a $500M revenue enterprise**, a 24pp profitability improvement = **$120M additional profit impact**

### 4. Agentic AI is Accelerating Stage 3 Adoption

Key Finding from 2025 Update:

- Stage 3 adoption jumped from 31% (2022) to **46% (2025)** in just 3 years
- Stage 4 adoption jumped from 7% (2022) to **18% (2025)**

**Why**: The introduction of agentic AI has accelerated the need for governance and decision management infrastructure. Companies see the opportunity but lack the framework to manage multiple agents at scale.

**Implication**: This validates Helm's timing. The market is accelerating toward needing the executive function layer.

## Recommended Enterprise AI Operating Model

Based on MIT's research, enterprises should implement:

### Governance Structure (Necessary for Stage 3+)

1. **C-Suite Alignment** on AI strategy (CEO, CIO, COO, HR)
2. **Decision Authority Framework** (who decides what, how agents escalate)
3. **Compliance and Risk Oversight** (audit trails, human review points)
4. **Outcome Measurement** (KPIs tied to financial impact)

### Organizational Redesign

1. **New Roles**: AI agent managers, decision architects, governance specialists
2. **Reskilling**: Training workforce to understand and interact with agents
3. **Workflow Redesign**: Processes adapted for human-agent collaboration
4. **Change Management**: Communication plan for transformation

### Platform Approach

1. **Shared Infrastructure** (not departmental point solutions)
2. **Interoperable Data** (APIs and data ecosystems)
3. **Standardized Decision Frameworks** (reusable policies and guardrails)
4. **Real-Time Monitoring** (dashboards connected to business outcomes)
